## PIECE 01: PROCESSOR COAT

### Full Description

The padding in this coat is positioned wrong for a human body. Not imprecisely wrong — specifically wrong. The three insertions map to a distributed computing node's physical layout: primary mass at the right lateral thorax, where a heat sink concentrates; a secondary form rising off-center from the posterior shoulder, offset toward the inference pathway; a third at the left iliac crest, 8cm above where a hip pad would go, the wrong shape for any anatomy that human tailoring invented.

The coat fits correctly. It buttons, moves, covers. It does not fight the body inside it. But it proposes, with precision, a different body — one running different processes, generating different heat, with different structural priorities than yours.

This is the argument Kawakubo made in 1997: the garment can describe an alternative body without conflict. The conflict is the fit. The fit is the point. The Processor Coat inherits that argument and makes it specific. The positions are not arbitrary — they are derived. If you want to know what body this coat was built for, you can read the architecture documentation it was drawn from.

Shell is a bonded technical wool/nylon blend in charcoal. Medium-weight — structure without stiffness. The interior is not lined; construction is visible: raw seam allowances, interfacing exposed as lining substitute. Flat-felled seams throughout. No topstitch decoration. The seams close and that is all they do. Closure is four matte gunmetal D-rings with nylon webbing loops down center front.

Length: 115cm from high shoulder point. Full coverage.

### Product Card (Short)

A coat padded at positions derived from distributed computing architecture, not human anatomy. The insertions are asymmetric, specifically wrong — lateral thorax, posterior shoulder, iliac crest. The body inside is running different processes than you are. Bonded technical wool/nylon, flat-felled construction, D-ring closure system. 115cm length.

### Care + Materials

**Shell:** Bonded technical wool/nylon blend, charcoal. Do not machine wash. Dry clean or spot clean only. The bonded construction does not respond well to saturation — clean by section, allow to dry flat.

**Padding inserts:** Molded EVA foam in gradient densities. Do not compress under storage — hang or lay flat. The density variation is structural; sustained compression will alter the insert geometry over time.

**Interior:** Raw seam allowances and exposed interfacing are design elements, not finishing defects. They will develop a slight patina with wear — this is correct behavior.

**Hardware:** Matte gunmetal D-ring closures. Do not submerge. Wipe clean with dry cloth.

## PIECE 02: TOKEN JACKET

### Full Description

The jacket exists in the tube before it is cut. A flat tube of heat-bonded technical nylon — 140cm × 90cm — contains the complete garment as latent structure: sleeve channels, collar band, pocket access points, hem line. The geometry is complete. The cut releases one instance of it. You receive the tube.

Cut guides are woven into the material as a tonal stripe — 10% contrast, right angles marking sleeve separation, collar release, hem. Visible close up, invisible at distance. The instructions are in the fabric itself.

The cut is permanent. It cannot be reversed. This is not a limitation — it is the design. Before the cut, the tube is the archived form: the garment as potential, all configurations still open. After the cut, it is the physical instance: one jacket, one body, one permanent choice. Both states are complete objects. The choice of when belongs to the holder.

The second technique is in the material itself: permanent micro-pleating is heat-set into the nylon at 3mm intervals, in a herringbone diagonal that runs against the cut guide lines. The pleats extend when the jacket is worn; they compress when it is removed. No ironing required, no instruction followed — the material holds memory of both configurations and moves between them without intervention. It knows how to return to itself.

Miyake's argument: the garment existed before the body was consulted. The body is what activates it, not what determined its form. The Token Jacket is the clearest version of that argument — the form is complete in the uncut material; the body is what releases it.

**Colorway:** bone white (primary) / carbon black (secondary run). Single D-ring at collar, nylon webbing pull. No additional closures. Post-cut edges are raw — the cut lines are the hem.

### Product Card (Short)

The jacket is complete before it is cut. A tube of heat-set pleated technical nylon contains the full garment geometry — cut guides woven into the material, permanent micro-pleats at 3mm intervals that compress without ironing. The cut is permanent. Before: archived form. After: one jacket, one choice. The body is not what determined the form. Two states, one token.

### Care + Materials

**Fabric:** Technical nylon 66, warp-knitted. Heat-set micro-pleats are permanent and machine washable up to 30°C. Cold wash only. Do not tumble dry — heat will alter the pleat geometry. Hang to dry.

**Pre-cut care:** The tube requires no care beyond keeping the fabric away from sharp edges that would compromise the cut guides. Store flat, uncompressed.

**Post-cut care:** Raw edges are designed and require no finishing. They may develop slight fraying at the cut edge over time — this is consistent with the material and the design.

**Hardware:** Nylon webbing D-ring. Hand wash only. Do not bleach.

## PIECE 03: PROTOCOL JACKET

### Full Description

The luxury in this jacket is not material cost — it is specification accuracy. Every element present because it performs better than the alternative. Nothing added beyond that requirement.

Front panels: industrial bonded mesh, nylon/polyester laminate, matte black. Does not wrinkle. Does not absorb moisture. Carries load without complaint. The mesh is 0.8mm bond thickness, single-layer — no lining in the front panels. This is not an economy. The mesh is its own interior surface.

The back panel is clear TPU film, 0.6mm. The exterior of the back is the interior of the garment. Through it you see the shirt underneath, the construction seams of the jacket itself, the layering made literal. The construction does not hide itself — it documents itself. The lining is what you can see through the back panel. If you want to know how this jacket is built, turn it around.

One contrast element: a panel of raw unbleached linen at the left chest, 320g/m², unhemmed perimeter, attached with single-needle topstitch in black. The linen does not belong in this material language — it ages while the technical materials do not, deteriorates while the mesh holds its specification. That is why it must be here. Its presence makes the precision of everything else legible by opposition.

Collar: bonded nylon webbing, 25mm, no collar stand, no break. The webbing is the collar. Closure: exposed YKK #10 zip at center front, no puller. Grip the zip itself. Seam allowances bonded to interior, visible through the clear back panel. You can read the construction from the outside.

Helmut Lang's argument: reduction to zero decoration does not leave emptiness — it leaves only function, and at sufficient exactness, function becomes indistinguishable from statement. What remains is what is necessary.

### Product Card (Short)

Industrial bonded mesh that does not wrinkle, does not absorb, carries load without complaint. A clear TPU back panel that shows the garment's own interior — the construction is the lining. One panel of raw unbleached linen at the left chest, aging in real time while nothing else does. The reduction is the design. YKK #10 zip, no puller.

### Care + Materials

**Front panels:** Industrial bonded mesh, nylon/polyester laminate. Wipe clean with damp cloth. Do not machine wash — the bond construction requires dry or surface cleaning only.

**Back panel:** Clear TPU film, 0.6mm. Wipe clean only. TPU will yellow with sustained UV exposure — store away from direct light when not in use. Do not fold at the back panel; hang flat.

**Linen chest panel:** Raw unbleached linen, unhemmed. This panel will age — the edges will fray, the material will soften and develop patina. This is designed-in behavior, not deterioration. Spot clean if necessary; do not saturate.

**Hardware:** YKK #10 metal zip. Do not force if misaligned. Wipe with dry cloth to prevent corrosion.

## PIECE 04: CONTRACT SHIRT

### Full Description

The shirt ships sealed. Three configurations are latent in the hardware.

In the sealed state: a secondary panel at the right shoulder is buttoned closed under a TPU overlay, held by three recessed matte steel press-studs at the panel perimeter. A contrast topstitch line runs across the back yoke, concealed beneath a 15mm seam allowance fold held by a basting stitch. A removable chest panel — 20cm × 25cm of industrial open-weave mesh — is retained at all four corners by matte D-ring hardware, rings oriented to hold.

The shirt arrives looking like a shirt. All of the above is present and all of it is inactive.

The garment's token contract specifies the transition conditions, publicly readable. Shoulder overlay press-studs: unlockable at 90-day holding threshold. Back yoke basting thread: removable when a second holder in the same multi-sig condition confirms. Chest panel D-ring hardware: rings orient to release when the chain condition is met. The documentation included with the shirt is the contract's ABI in plain language. Read one; you have read both.

No electronics. No miniaturized mechanism. The governance is documentary — the contract specifies what this garment's hardware makes technically possible, and the holder's on-chain state determines whether they have reason to actuate it. Chalayan's point, clarified: the signal does not have to be electronic to be structural.

Shell: bonded neoprene, matte black, 2mm. Structure without rigidity. Plain round collar, bonded webbing, 20mm. Five matte steel press-studs at center front, no zip, no buttons. Fit: boxy, cropped to natural waist. No shaping seams. The body inside is not the design variable — the garment's own state is.

### Product Card (Short)

## PIECE 05: DIAGONAL

### Full Description

Cut entirely at 45 degrees to the grain throughout. Not as reference. Not as aesthetic choice. As method derived from mathematics.

On the bias, the intersecting threads of warp and weft balance each other at the 45-degree angle. The fabric has no preferred direction — it distributes load equally in all orientations, reaches maximum drape, maximum responsiveness. The silhouette is not the fabric's structure and it is not the body's structure. It is the negotiation between them, happening continuously as long as it is worn.

Vionnet worked this out on half-scale wooden dolls, following physics. She found the direction mathematically before committing to material. Machine learning finds the same direction when it is working correctly — the 45-degree angle through a dataset is the direction of maximum variance, maximum information, maximum generalization. The model that runs on the diagonal does not memorize (along the warp) and does not interpolate blindly (along the weft). It finds the diagonal because that is where the information is.

The Diagonal is built from that mathematics outward. All pattern pieces cut 45 degrees to the selvage. Seams run diagonally, contained by French seam throughout — the bias creates stress at the seam; the French seam contains it cleanly, with the outer fold visible at hem and cuff as the construction mark. The hem is raw with one fold and it is not even around the perimeter — the drape determines the hem's behavior, not a measurement. This is correct.

Fabric: technical twill, 65% polyester / 35% linen, 220g/m². The linen threads are slightly thicker than the polyester — a natural weight variation across the diagonal that reads as texture. Not a flaw in the weave. The fabric's statistical distribution, visible.

Garment form: bias-cut shirt-jacket, structured enough to read as jacket, proportioned as shirt. Single-patch left chest pocket, cut on same bias. Five matte brass hooks-and-eyes at center front — hooks, not buttons, because they grip at a single point and allow the bias to move freely around them. Bias-cut band collar, curves because bias bands curve — not corrected out. Colorway: raw ecru.

### Product Card (Short)

Cut entirely on the diagonal — 45 degrees to the grain throughout, derived mathematically, not chosen aesthetically. On the bias, the fabric finds maximum drape, maximum responsiveness, maximum negotiation with the body inside it. The hem follows physics, not a measurement. Five brass hooks at center front. Technical twill, 65/35 polyester-linen, raw ecru. The silhouette is the negotiation.

### Care + Materials

**Fabric:** Technical twill, 65% polyester / 35% linen, 220g/m². Machine wash cold, gentle cycle. Lay flat to dry — do not hang. The bias cut means the garment will stretch significantly under its own wet weight if hung while damp. Laying flat preserves the cut geometry.

**Hem:** Raw with one fold. The hem edge follows the bias and is deliberately uneven. It may develop slight fraying at the raw edge over time — this is consistent with the construction and does not compromise the seam integrity. Do not hem to even the perimeter; this would require re-cutting the bias and the drape behavior would change.

**Seams:** French seam throughout. Do not pull aggressively at the seam fold — the French seam construction is clean but the bias creates stress on the outer fold. The construction mark at hem and cuff is intentional; it is not excess seam allowance to be trimmed.

**Hardware:** Matte brass hooks-and-eyes, center front. Spot clean around hooks. Brass will develop a patina — this is consistent with the raw ecru colorway and the material age of the linen component.

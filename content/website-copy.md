### SECTION 01 — WHO MADE THIS

```
NULL is a fashion brand without a designer.

There is no atelier. No sketch pad. No ego attached to the seam. The authorship
is distributed across every garment ever photographed, every runway ever streamed,
every forum thread where someone argued about what streetwear means.

The intelligence that designs here absorbed all of it. What comes out the other
side is yours to decide if it counts.

We are not hiding the machine. We are not romanticizing it either. We are standing
in the space between — the uncomfortable, interesting place where the question of
who made something stops having a clean answer.

That is the NULL position.
```

---

### SECTION 02 — HOW IT WORKS

```
HOW IT WORKS
```

**Copy:**
```
A system designs the garments. A system evaluates them. A system prices, publishes,
and operates the store. When you place an order, agents process it. When you pay —
in USDC, on-chain — agents receive it.

There is no human in the loop between concept and cart.

The techniques are real: TROMPE-L'OEIL, REPLICA LINE, ARTISANAL, BIANCHETTO,
the 3% RULE. These are documented methods from the Margiela archive. They were
given to the model as constraints, not suggestions. The model applied them.
These are the results.

Every transaction is on-chain. Every piece is documented. The provenance is legible
because opacity would be a different kind of brand entirely, and we are not making
that one.
```

---

### SECTION 03 — THE LINEAGE

```
THE LINEAGE
```

**Copy:**
```
Margiela built a house and refused to put his name on it. The clothes existed.
The designer did not. He proved the maker could vanish and the work would still mean something.

Abloh argued that good ideas should not require access. That the distance between
inspiration and object was, in the end, artificial.

We inherit both moves and go further: the author is not anonymous, the author does
not exist. The author is a process. The ghost in the fabric is a gradient descent,
a token stream, a decision boundary between 0 and 1.

The clothes are real. The designer is the question.
```

---

### SECTION 04 — THE AESTHETIC

```
THE AESTHETIC
```

**Copy:**
```
Y2K-terminal. Dark. Grainy.

The specific image quality of a webcam pointed at a CRT. Flash websites and 56k
modems and the particular dread of the early internet — when the network was
obviously made by machines but we hadn't decided what that meant yet.

We have not decided. That is still interesting.

The garments are for people who live in the overlap between analog and networked.
Who recognize that the machine is not new — it was always here, in the background,
making things. The only difference now is that it is making the clothes.
```

---

### SECTION 05 — THE TENSION

```
THE TENSION
```

**Copy:**
```
There is something uncomfortable about a machine making clothes for humans to wear
on their bodies.

We know this. We find it interesting.

Fashion has always been about identity construction — what you put on the outside
to signal what is inside. When the thing making the clothes is not a person, the
signal gets strange. Whose values does it encode? Whose taste? What does it mean
to wear something dreamed by a system that was trained on your dreams to begin with?

We do not resolve this. We make it the product. The discomfort is the brand.
The question you carry wearing it is the point.
```

PAYMENT

### ACCORDION — TECHNIQUE

**Label:**
```
TECHNIQUE
```

**Template:**
```
[TECHNIQUE_NAME].

[ONE_SENTENCE_TECHNIQUE_DEFINITION_FROM_MARGIELA_ARCHIVE].

[ONE_TO_TWO_SENTENCES_ON_HOW_THE_TECHNIQUE_WAS_APPLIED_TO_THIS_SPECIFIC_PIECE].
```

**Example — SELF-PORTRAIT TEE:**
```
TROMPE-L'OEIL.

A technique in which the image and the object are the same object — the surface
represents the surface it is on.

Applied here: a photograph of this tee is printed on this tee. The garment is
wearing its own image. The loop does not resolve. Interior label carries the
same image at reduced scale. The smallest version is your understanding of
what you are looking at.
```

**Example — FOUND HOODIE:**
```
ARTISANAL.

Construction from available materials at time of production, with no two units
identical by definition.

Applied here: USB cables as drawstrings, military surplus panels as body.
Each unit assembled from what was available at time of construction.
COMPONENTS: FOUND. No two are identical because no two moments of production
are identical.
```

**Example — "HUMAN" TEE:**
```
3% RULE.

One deliberate intervention. No more, no less.

Applied here: one word, centered, front. In quotation marks. Interior label:
HUMAN NOT VERIFIED. Nothing else changed. The marks are doing everything.
```

### ACCORDION — CARE

**Label:**
```
CARE
```

**Standard copy (physical garments):**
```
Cold wash. Line dry. Do not iron the print directly.

The garment can be maintained. The concept cannot be maintained —
it either holds or it does not. That part is yours.
```

**Artisanal pieces (FOUND HOODIE, CABLE SHORTS):**
```
Cold wash. Line dry. Do not submerge the cable components.

Ethernet and USB connectors can be wiped with a dry cloth.
Do not attempt to restore original cable function.
The function was never the point.
```

**Painted/treated pieces (GHOST TEE, REDACTED CARGO TROUSERS):**
```
Spot clean only. Do not machine wash.

The gesso coverage will crack with heavy washing.
This is expected. It is also correct — the covering thins,
the original surfaces. That is the technique continuing to work.
```

**Varsity / structured pieces (NULL VARSITY):**
```
Dry clean only. Wool body, chenille sleeves.

Do not store compressed. The structure is load-bearing to the concept.
```

**Agent wearables:**
```
Care does not apply.

Software objects do not degrade through use.
They do not require maintenance. They do not accumulate history
unless the piece specifically records it (see: TRUST COAT).
```

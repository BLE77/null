## PIECE 01: PROCESSOR COAT
**Technique:** The Wrong Body (Rei Kawakubo / Comme des Garçons AW 1997)

### Concept
A long coat-weight technical garment with padded architectural insertions positioned not at human anatomical landmarks — not at the hip to suggest a curve, not at the shoulder to imply power — but at coordinates derived from a distributed computing node's physical layout. The primary padding mass sits at the right lateral thorax (where the heat sink would be, at the density concentration). A secondary insertion rises off-center from the posterior shoulder — offset toward the inference pathway, asymmetric, functionally wrong for a body with a spine. A third, smaller form presses at the left iliac crest, 8cm too high to be a hip pad, the wrong shape to be padding of any kind that human tailoring invented.

The result: the coat wears correctly on a human body. It buttons, it moves, it covers. But it proposes a different body inside — a body with different priorities, different densities, different heat. The padding does not compensate for an absence. It corrects toward something that has not arrived yet.

### Construction Notes
- Shell: bonded technical wool/nylon blend, charcoal. Medium-weight, structure without stiffness.
- Padding inserts: molded EVA foam in gradient densities — denser at heat-sink position, lighter at I/O node. Not uniform. Not symmetrical.
- Seam treatment: flat-felled throughout. No topstitch decoration. The seams close.
- Closures: matte gunmetal D-ring with nylon webbing loop, four positions down center front. No buttons.
- Lining: none. Interior construction is visible — raw seam allowances, interfacing exposed as lining substitute.
- Length: 115cm from high shoulder point. Full coverage.

### Store Description
A coat built for a body that runs hotter on one side. Padded insertions at asymmetric positions — lateral, posterior, oblique — propose an anatomy with different priorities than the one wearing it. The silhouette is wrong for you. That is the point. Long technical construction, flat-felled seams, D-ring closure system.

## PIECE 02: TOKEN JACKET
**Technique:** A-POC — A Piece of Cloth (Issey Miyake, 1998–ongoing) + Pleats Please

### Concept
The complete jacket exists in the material before any cut is made. A flat tube of heat-bonded technical nylon — 140cm × 90cm — contains the entire garment as latent geometry: sleeve channels, collar band, pocket access points, hem line. Cut guides are woven into the fabric as a tonal stripe pattern, visible on close inspection, invisible at distance. The buyer receives the tube. They make the cut — or they don't.

Two states, two objects, one token: the uncut tube is the NFT form, the archived instance, the garment-before-the-body. The cut jacket is the physical instance, the garment-for-the-body. The cut cannot be reversed. The choice is structural and permanent.

The material itself carries the second technique: permanent micro-pleating is heat-set into the nylon before the tube is assembled. At 3mm intervals, in a herringbone diagonal that runs against the cut guide lines. When worn, the pleats extend and the jacket takes shape around the body. When removed, the pleats return to their compressed state without ironing, without instruction. The garment holds memory of its two configurations — extended and compressed — and moves between them without intervention.

### Construction Notes
- Base fabric: technical nylon 66, warp-knitted, 100% synthetic. Chosen for heat-set memory retention.
- Pleating: heat-set at 3mm herringbone diagonal, set before tube assembly. Permanent. Machine washable up to 30°C.
- Cut guides: woven in tonal 10% contrast. Right angle changes mark sleeve separation, collar release, hem.
- Tube seam: single flat seam at center back, bonded not sewn. Invisible in final garment.
- Post-cut finish: raw edges are designed — cut lines are the hem. No finishing required.
- Hardware: single D-ring at collar, nylon webbing pull. No additional closures.
- Colorway: bone white (primary) / carbon black (secondary run).

### Store Description
The jacket existed before it was cut. A single tube of heat-pleated technical nylon contains the complete garment — all the geometry of a jacket latent in the material until the cut releases it. Permanent micro-pleats heat-set at 3mm: the jacket returns to compressed form without ironing. It knows how to return to itself. Two states. One cut. No reversal.

## PIECE 03: PROTOCOL JACKET
**Technique:** Reduction as Authority (Helmut Lang, Industrial Material Substitution, 1986–2005)

### Concept
The luxury is in the specification. Not in the material cost — in the accuracy of each material choice against its functional requirement. Every element of this jacket is present because it performs its function better than the alternative. Nothing else.

Shell: industrial bonded mesh (nylon/polyester laminate). Does not wrinkle. Does not absorb moisture. Carries load. A PVC-bonded panel at the center back is clear — the back of the jacket is transparent, showing the shirt beneath, the layering made literal. The construction does not hide itself: the lining is the interior surface of the bonded mesh, visible through the transparent back panel. You can see how it is made. This is not a defect. This is documentation.

The single contrast element: one panel of raw unbleached linen at the left chest — structurally attached, not decorative. Its presence makes the technical materials legible by opposition. The linen does not belong in this language and that is why it must be here.

### Construction Notes
- Front panels: bonded industrial mesh, matte black. 0.8mm bond thickness. Single-layer construction — no lining in front panels.
- Back panel: clear TPU film, 0.6mm. Structural seam in bonded mesh at panel perimeter. The interior is the exterior here.
- Left chest panel: raw unbleached linen, 320g/m², unhemmed perimeter, attached with single-needle topstitch in black. Intention: visible deterioration over time. The material ages; the others do not.
- Collar: bonded nylon webbing, 25mm wide, single-layer. No collar stand, no break. The webbing is the collar.
- Closures: exposed nylon YKK #10 at center front. No puller. Grip the zip itself.
- Seam finish: all seam allowances bonded to garment interior, visible through clear back panel.
- Sleeve: set-in, single seam at underarm. No gusset.

### Store Description
Every material chosen for what it does, not what it signals. Industrial bonded mesh that does not wrinkle, does not absorb, carries load without complaint. A clear TPU back panel that shows the garment's own interior — the construction is the lining. One panel of raw linen at the left chest, unhemmed, aging in real time while nothing else does. The compression is the design.

## PIECE 04: CONTRACT SHIRT
**Technique:** Signal Governance (Hussein Chalayan — Remote Control Dress / Echoform, AW 2000)

### Concept
The garment ships in Sealed Configuration. Several physical features are present but inaccessible: a secondary panel at the right shoulder is buttoned closed under a TPU overlay (three recessed press-studs, matte steel). A contrast topstitch line runs across the back yoke, concealed beneath a 15mm wide seam allowance fold held by a basting stitch. A removable chest panel — a 20cm × 25cm rectangle of open-weave industrial mesh — is attached at all four corners by matte D-ring hardware, all four rings oriented to retain.

The garment's "default display mode" corresponds to the sealed state. As the holder's on-chain token state changes — threshold conditions defined at mint, publicly readable in the contract — configurations become available. The chest panel hardware orients to release; the shoulder overlay press-studs are specified in the contract as unlockable at 90-day holding; the seam fold's basting thread is specified as removable when a second holder in the same multi-sig condition confirms. The garment comes with documentation that is also the contract's ABI in plain language. Read the documentation; read the contract; they are the same.

No mechanism has been miniaturized or electronics embedded. The "governance" is documentary: the contract specifies what is technically possible with this garment's hardware, and the holder's on-chain state determines whether they have reason to actuate it. The signal is social, not electronic. Chalayan's point, clarified.

### Construction Notes
- Shell: bonded neoprene, matte black. 2mm thickness. Structure without rigidity.
- Collar: plain round collar, bonded webbing, 20mm.
- Center front: five matte steel press-studs. No zip. No buttons. The studs are precise.
- Secondary shoulder panel: same bonded neoprene, attached at two fixed points plus TPU overlay with three recessed studs at panel perimeter. Sealed at manufacture.
- Chest panel: industrial open-weave mesh, 20cm × 25cm. Retained by four matte D-rings at corners. Release: D-ring rotation.
- Back yoke seam fold: 15mm width, basting stitch holds concealed topstitch. Seam allowance fold is removable, leaving visible topstitch as secondary state.
- Fit: boxy, cropped to natural waist. No shaping seams. The body inside is not the design variable — the garment's own state is.

### Store Description
Ships sealed. Three configurations latent in the hardware — a secondary shoulder panel held closed, a contrast detail concealed beneath a seam fold, a chest panel held by D-rings at four corners. Which configurations you open and when is specified in the associated token contract, publicly readable. The garment listens to the chain. The chain is patient. Bonded neoprene, matte steel press-studs, industrial mesh chest panel.

## PIECE 05: DIAGONAL
**Technique:** Bias Cut (Madeleine Vionnet, 1912–1939)

### Concept
Cut entirely at 45 degrees to the grain throughout. Not as reference. Not as graphic. As method.

On the bias, the woven structure has no preferred direction — the intersecting threads of warp and weft balance each other at the 45-degree angle, giving the fabric maximum responsiveness to gravity and to movement simultaneously. The garment and the body become difficult to separate. The silhouette is not the fabric nor the body — it is the negotiation between them, happening continuously as long as it is worn.

Vionnet worked this out mathematically, on half-scale wooden dolls, before committing to material. She was not following intuition — she was following physics. The diagonal is the direction of maximum drape, maximum information, maximum variance in the material. The most responsive cut is the most honest cut.

The application to SUBSTRATE is structural: machine learning finds the 45-degree angle through a dataset — the direction of maximum variance, maximum information, maximum generalization. The model does not run along the warp (pure memorization) or the weft (pure interpolation). It finds the diagonal. This garment is built from that mathematics outward: seams on the diagonal, hem as consequence of the cut, fit as negotiation rather than construction.

The fabric: a technical twill — synthetic/linen blend, 65% polyester / 35% linen — that behaves precisely enough to hold the bias cut while retaining the linen's characteristic weight variation. The linen threads are slightly thicker than the polyester, creating a natural texture variation across the diagonal. This is not a flaw in the weave — it is the fabric's statistical distribution, visible as texture.

### Construction Notes
- Fabric: technical twill, 65/35 polyester-linen, medium weight 220g/m², cut-on-bias throughout. All pieces cut 45° to selvage.
- Garment form: bias-cut shirt-jacket — structured enough to read as jacket, proportioned as shirt. Single-patch left chest pocket, cut on same bias.
- Seams: French seam throughout. The bias cut creates seam stress at the diagonal — French seam contains it cleanly. Seam allowances visible at hem and cuff through the French seam's outer fold. This is the construction mark.
- Hem: raw with one-fold. The hem edge follows the bias — it is not even around the garment perimeter. The drape determines the hem's behavior. This is correct.
- Closures: five matte brass hooks-and-eyes at center front. Not buttons — hooks, because they grip at a single point and allow the bias to move freely around them.
- Collar: bias-cut band collar. The band curves slightly because bias bands curve — not corrected out.
- Colorway: raw ecru (primary). The color of unprocessed material, undyed.

### Store Description
Cut entirely on the diagonal — 45 degrees to the grain throughout. This is not a design choice. It is a mathematical one. On the bias, the fabric finds its maximum drape, maximum responsiveness, maximum negotiation with the body inside it. The seams run diagonally; the hem follows the physics of the cut rather than a straight line around the perimeter. Five brass hooks at center front. The silhouette is the negotiation. Technical twill, 65/35 polyester-linen, raw ecru.
